Land Use Management

Napa County Vineyard Site Selection

Tools ArcGIS Pro
Techniques Raster Analysis, IDW, Map Algebra
Result 1,270+ acres identified

The Challenge

Napa County is one of the world's premier wine-growing regions, but not every acre is suitable for vineyards. Successful viticulture depends on specific environmental conditions including soil quality, drainage, elevation, slope, wind exposure, and flood risk.

The goal was to identify optimal vineyard locations by analyzing multiple environmental criteria simultaneously using raster-based GIS analysis.

The Approach

This project marked my first major work with raster data, which uses a grid model to represent continuous environmental variables like elevation, temperature, and wind speed.

Site Suitability Criteria

Optimal vineyard locations needed to meet all of the following:

  • Outside floodplain zones to minimize crop destruction risk
  • Over 100 meters from streams and water bodies
  • Favorable slope (112-137 degrees or flat terrain)
  • Low wind speeds (25 mph or less)
  • Adequate soil depth and drainage
  • Higher hillshade values for optimal sunlight exposure

Technical Methods

The analysis employed several GIS techniques:

  • Vector to Raster Conversion: Transformed floodplain polygons into raster format for overlay analysis
  • Inverse Distance Weighted (IDW) Interpolation: Created continuous surfaces for climate and soil variables from sample points
  • Aspect Analysis: Evaluated slope direction to identify areas with optimal sun exposure
  • Binary Reclassification: Converted continuous data into simple suitable (1) vs. unsuitable (0) classifications
  • Map Algebra: Combined all criteria layers to identify areas meeting every requirement

Results

The analysis identified approximately 1,270.9 acres of land suitable for vineyard development. This was calculated by:

  1. Counting 51,432 suitable cells in the final raster
  2. Multiplying by cell size (10m x 10m = 100 square meters per cell)
  3. Converting to acres (5,143,200 sq meters = 1,270.9 acres)

When public land restrictions were added, suitable acreage decreased to 720.2 acres, demonstrating how policy constraints affect development potential.

Practical Insights

Beyond the raw acreage numbers, the analysis revealed valuable location-specific insights:

  • Urban proximity matters: Areas near the town of Salvador offer advantages like access to labor pools and proximity to high schools for potential employees
  • Rural trade-offs: More remote sites may lack workforce access but offer lower traffic and more open space for expansion
  • Scale considerations: Lower resolution data shows county-wide patterns but misses local features that affect site-level decisions

Reflections

This project helped me understand when to use raster versus vector analysis. Raster is ideal for continuous environmental variables across large areas, while vector works better for discrete features in localized studies.

For example: planning a national rail system would benefit from raster analysis of elevation and terrain, while placing a new subway station requires vector analysis of street networks, buildings, and population density.
