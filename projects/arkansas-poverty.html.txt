            <h2>Key Findings</h2>
            
            <div class="key-finding">
                <h3>Population Bias in County-Level Analysis</h3>
                <p>The proportional symbols map at the county level primarily reflects population distribution rather than poverty concentration. Counties containing Arkansas's largest cities—Fayetteville in the northwest and Little Rock, the state capital—show the largest symbols due to their high total populations, not necessarily higher poverty rates.</p>
            </div>

            <div class="analysis-content">
                <h3>The California Effect</h3>
                <p>This phenomenon mirrors presidential election results where California yields the most Republican votes of any state despite consistently voting Democratic by large margins. Similarly, populous Arkansas counties may contain the most people in poverty while simultaneously housing the most affluent residents—a direct consequence of population size rather than poverty rate.</p>
            </div>

            <div class="key-finding">
                <h3>Census Tract Advantages</h3>
                <p>Census tracts, being more uniform in population size, provide clearer insights into actual poverty concentration. The census tract analysis reveals higher proportions of poverty in the eastern portion of Arkansas, demonstrated through both larger symbols and more densely concentrated dots.</p>
            </div>

            <div class="analysis-content">
                <h3>Scale Matters</h3>
                <p>State-level analysis without jurisdictional boundaries provides limited context and can be misleading. These visualizations would be more meaningful when comparing Arkansas to other states at a smaller scale, where relative differences become more interpretable.</p>
            </div>
        </section>

        <section class="section">
            <h2>Methodological Insights</h2>
            <h3>Effective Geographic Units</h3>
            <p>For measuring poverty distribution within Arkansas, census tracts prove more effective than counties. The more uniform population sizes across census tracts reduce population bias and allow both dot density and proportional symbol maps to accurately represent poverty concentration.</p>

            <h3>Visualization Selection</h3>
            <p>The choice between visualization methods significantly impacts interpretation. While proportional symbols effectively show absolute numbers, they can obscure rates and percentages. Combining multiple methods—such as dot density with proportional symbols—provides a more comprehensive understanding of spatial patterns.</p>

            <h3>Context and Comparison</h3>
            <p>Geographic analysis requires appropriate context. Statewide data presented without jurisdictional comparison or interstate benchmarks offers limited insight. Effective poverty mapping requires either intra-state jurisdictional analysis or inter-state comparative frameworks.</p>
        </section>
